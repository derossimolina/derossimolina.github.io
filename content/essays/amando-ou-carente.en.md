Psychology experts say that solitude can give wings to your creativity. That may be true, but sooner or later you will feel the urge to share your time, your activities, and what you love --- or don't --- with someone.

Even if it takes a while, human beings are social creatures. We relate to people all the time. We could never become an island in a sea of people and their frantic connections through social media, WhatsApp, messengers, and the rest.

Sharing experiences --- bizarre or not --- is universal. On Facebook, for example, we see users asking where to buy a birthday cake, announcing a separation from their spouse, mourning the loss of a loved one, or even seeking something a bit more spicy with the neighbor next door.

For the purists out there, this might seem like an invasion of privacy or, at its most extreme, a case of wild self-exposure. But if we look deeper, it is simply a way of sharing, of being social.

Sure, you might think that this idea of being social in front of a computer or smartphone is nothing but nonsense --- that the real thing is to go to a bar, meet someone interesting, and at the very least forge a great friendship. And you might be right.

What truly afflicts people today is not simply lacking a friend, but the ache of having no one with whom to share their worries, anxieties, sorrows, and grievances. This ache cannot be relieved by friendship alone, no matter how deep. Friends --- despite the saying --- may not always be available, and inevitably we find ourselves alone with our existential ghosts. This relentless, constant pursuit of new experiences, new friends, new loves, new passions, and something else that is new keeps us from ever feeling truly fulfilled.

That is why, when we end up shackled by the "handcuffs of love," it feels as though everything could crumble in an instant --- and I say this from experience.

The urgency to have someone deeply intimate and close, someone with whom we can share our struggles, makes relationships more intense but also more fragile. So we see marriages that "had everything going for them" falling apart, couples betraying each other, constant fights, and a sea of problems piling up on top of what was once so good.

After getting knocked around enough, as they say, I learned that any relationship must be built on solid pillars. The adolescent crushes --- even though I am already an adult --- should be set aside for something more concrete. Yet even when thinking more realistically than romantically, I still find myself asking whether what I feel is loneliness or love.

It may sound sentimental, but love "sees nothing, accepts everything, endures everything, feels no jealousy, envy, or pain." When we enter a relationship and experience it as a way to fill the void of having no one to share our troubles with, we are not truly loving --- we are just lonely.

When we are merely lonely, a relationship with another person "doesn't take root." We end up discovering that the compatibilities don't exist, that the way she puts away clothes bothers us, that the way he buys fruit irritates us, and it all heads toward the end.

A relationship forged by the loneliness of one --- or both --- becomes toxic because there is no real emotional bond, only a passing emotional depression that, over time, gives the other person a sense of ownership. The moments that once seemed beautiful and wonderful become tedious. In short, loneliness suffocates the true feeling of love.

These days we rarely see couples who have been together for more than ten years. I cannot say it is entirely a matter of fashion, but I can assure you that many of the couples who stay together year after year do so because they bear with each other, flaws and all. Loneliness cannot tolerate flaws, delays, or demands, because it is selfish.

The need to always be near someone, to send mountains of messages, to call every single day, or to flood their inbox with a couple of sweet words already reveals --- beyond insecurity --- loneliness. That is why we must be very careful when entering a romantic relationship with anyone, so that we do not become dependent on having someone.

Even though contemporary psychology has not conclusively identified what we feel when we are in love, we must exercise extreme caution. So that what we call love is not merely loneliness, and after a few days, months, or perhaps even years, we find ourselves in front of the computer, Facebook open, tears in our eyes, typing to the four corners of the world: "I am so alone."
