The Brazilian singer Cazuza once declared in his song "Ideologia" that ideology was something he lacked and desperately needed. But in truth, we all carry an ideology --- and I do not mean within the tired left-right dichotomy. Norberto Bobbio, the political philosopher, even "decreed" the end of that dichotomy, which nonetheless insists on lingering in everyday life. Contrary to what Cazuza sang, ideology is present in our lives at every moment, shaping the way we see the world, its things, and its people.

It is from this worldview that we act, thereby molding our personality. This is precisely what John T. Jost brings to his research on political psychology: a greater affinity for certain values shapes our personality through ideology. Put another way, when someone decides to champion a cause, they are displaying their worldview and, consequently, their personality before society. This is also reflected in consumption, in family relations, at work --- everywhere.

In this light, when a politician gives a speech, they reveal how they see the world, society, and life, and they act on the basis of these precepts in a psychological manner. Precepts such as empathy, fear of death, and social relationships determine whether that politician is more open to innovation or resistant to change, whether they reject inequality or accept it. This shapes their attitudes across the most diverse situations, from buying a bread roll at the bakery to negotiating with international partners. In the same way, just like politicians, we all have our own ideology, revealed by the way we act in the most varied everyday situations.

When a person's actions reflect misogyny, xenophobia, aporophobia --- a disdain for the poor --- or homophobia, they are not merely stating an opinion. They are declaring that this is how they think, regardless of where they are or who they are with.
