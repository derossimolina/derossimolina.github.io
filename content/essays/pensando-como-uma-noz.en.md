Whenever someone thinks in a misguided way, we say they have a brain the size of a pea. The comparison is obvious: peas are small and green, and hundreds of them fit inside a tin can. We do not even bother counting them --- we measure them by weight.

When dealing with someone who has a big head but an empty one, we say they have the head of a walnut. Yes, that walnut we eat on festive occasions and that costs a small fortune. A walnut is large but light. We count them by the unit, rarely taking more than a few home, and to discover what is inside we must crack the shell in a rather crude fashion --- sometimes with a hammer, sometimes with a nutcracker.

Public officials are often compared to peas and walnuts, and not without reason. Day after day, year after year, they commit the same blunders as their predecessors. They complicate things to the point that people take to the streets in protest. Yet there they are again, asking for your vote --- because we let things go down this road.

Any sensible, committed administrator strives to deliver the best results for their constituents. Public officials, however, tend to do the opposite, making the ordinary citizen's daily life even more complicated. Without taking sides, the public official who comes from politics rather than from management believes in fantastical claims and preaches to a passionate audience. They do not practice governance; they practice politics.

The actual work of public management falls to civil servants, but even that is not always possible. By law, at least in Brazil, public servants are selected through competitive examinations. In other words, you just have to pass a test. Whoever studies the most wins the coveted government position. Your previous profession, your training, your drive --- none of it matters. You just have to pass the test.

Those who take a civil service exam typically have a single goal in mind: salary and job security. No one expects to take such an exam hoping to change fields or serve the public good. The standard public employee --- for there are always exceptions --- is not concerned with the greater picture, but with their own. The problems of the general population matter little; what matters are the problems of their own group.

The people are left gazing at the sky, pleading for a new public leader. But the cycle repeats itself --- we always end up choosing those thinkers with walnut-sized brains. And why does this happen?

In this book we will dissect the inner workings of party politics and public management alike, in order to finally discover why on earth we always choose the worst public leaders.
