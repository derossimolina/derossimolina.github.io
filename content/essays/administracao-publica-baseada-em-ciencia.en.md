When we think of science, we tend to picture people in white lab coats surrounded by test tubes — and that is indeed the most common image. Yet there are many fields of science whose researchers never set foot in a laboratory. Administration is one of them.

To administer is not simply to give orders. It involves a body of principles and theories that map out which paths to follow and which to avoid. Through the work of F. W. Taylor and Henry Fayol (engineers), Max Weber (sociologist), and Emile Durkheim (psychologist) — along with allied disciplines such as Economics and Accounting — what had been considered an "art" in previous centuries was given scientific grounding.

Like every science, Administration is evidence-based: everything must be demonstrated through research following the scientific method and requires sufficient data before any decision is made. Running companies, small businesses, or governments should always rest on planning, calculations, spreadsheets, and a great deal of reflection.

Public Administration should be no different, since it draws on all the tenets of Administration while adding practical strategies focused on the relationships among social actors, from ordinary citizens to public servants. This approach seeks to bring a fresh perspective to public-sector management through Open Strategy — transparency in the design of public policy.

These new strategies can broaden social participation, promote equality, and drive innovation, bringing twenty-first-century Administration, grounded in science, into the public sphere.
