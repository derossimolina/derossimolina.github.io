The paradigm of Brazilian Public Administration has stalled in time. A few days ago I took part in a seminar organized by the OECD where digital solutions implemented during Covid-19 — and beyond the pandemic itself — were discussed. Panelists from Colombia, Mexico, Costa Rica, and Brazil participated, all members of their respective national governments.

Setting aside the behind-the-scenes realities known only to each country's nationals, the enthusiasm was palpable when the Colombian panelist began to speak. In Colombia, government digitalization is advancing at a remarkable pace, with an open government proposal on the near horizon. It is no surprise that one of her chief concerns was training public servants to help them understand the need for data transparency and closer engagement with citizens.

In Brazil, the situation is moving in reverse. The proposed digital government law actually adds bureaucracy by requiring a digital certificate to access public services offered online. Moreover, it centralizes various services on a single platform that is, in practice, uncoordinated — services remain decentralized, hindering transparency and usability. All of this in the midst of a pandemic and at a cost of 1.5 billion dollars in the past year alone, according to the representative from the Ministry of Economy.

All of this can be summed up in one word: managerialism. Unfortunately, Brazil still runs its public management — and by extension its public policies — based on the principles of New Public Management (Hood, 1991; Hudon and Rouillard, 2015). Heavily influenced by managerialist and neoliberal thinking, this approach constrains the construction of alternatives through intense bureaucratic pressure, rigid hierarchies, and above all the treatment of public administration as if it were a private enterprise (Diefenbach, 2009).

Transparent proposals such as Open Strategy (Birkinshaw, 2017), which could be applied to public administration, are relegated to the background. This feeds corporatist tendencies, nurtures corruption (Matias-Pereira, 2008), and promotes an unnecessary dynamic of coopetition (Tidstrom and Rajala, 2016). The most fundamental stakeholder of public management — the citizen — is trapped within this process, treated as a customer to be served. And when that citizen lacks the resources, whether financial or legal, they are simply left behind.

Citizen-oriented public policies are essential, but an equally urgent shift in the managerial paradigm of Public Administration is needed — moving from a purely functionalist orientation to a radical humanist one (Burrell and Morgan, 2019). In this framework, the best approach does not center on consensus among social actors but rather on the power that emerges from those actors, taking into account the antagonism between them that arises from pluralism (Mouffe, 2005). In other words, a "New Radical Public Management."
