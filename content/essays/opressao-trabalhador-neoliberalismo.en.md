In contemporary management theory, it is commonly taught that labor is merely a resource. The very term "human resources" exists for a reason: it treats people as machines conditioned to produce the maximum output with the minimum of guarantees. Some postmodern philosophers, among them Jean-Francois Lyotard, connect this practice to the concept of "performativity" — that is, performing within the social arena in ways that drive every action toward maximum profitability.

Starting from this premise, companies organize themselves around the maximization of profit at the expense of the person behind the work. In other words, they oppress the worker in order to subjugate them in the name of peak returns. With rare exceptions, this modus operandi has its roots in the Fordist and Taylorist models of understanding productivity: the human being as the archetype of a machine. But people have feelings; they suffer, they get hurt, they tire. They are not machines.

And so companies feel the need to demand that workers "wear the company jersey," regardless of the personal aspirations of those who trade their sweat for corporate profitability. Today, this wearing of the jersey manifests in exhausting hours spent in front of a computer during remote work, or in enduring unsafe conditions just to keep a job, among other things. Yet the direct oppression seen in the past is shifting.

Now the approach plays out on social media, exploiting workers' appetite for rewards. It is not uncommon to see profile photos featuring mugs, notebooks, and other branded items presented as "perks." Meanwhile, working hours keep growing; the pressure for results — especially during the pandemic — became alarming. Countless professionals reported that their workload increased with the adoption of remote work, producing something like the "total institutionalization" of employment in one's life.

In other words, the worker has become an "entrepreneurial subject," to use the term coined by Pierre Dardot and Christian Laval, where the conduct of private life mirrors the management of an organization. People set goals, hire consultants (for everything from career planning to child-rearing), and establish rigid deadlines (for friendships, for marriage) — all with personal and professional advancement as the ultimate aim. Those who are less "capable" (referring not to formal qualifications but to what are called soft skills) are pushed to the margins of this process.
