Today the internet is a basic necessity — something unthinkable just twenty years ago. Nearly every Brazilian has access to the web, whether through mobile devices, computers, or even schools and universities. Major companies now hire exclusively through websites; we summon rides and order food through apps. If Alvin Toffler could have foreseen such an evolution, I believe he would have called it the "final wave."

Yet governments move slowly through this revolution. Still gripped by the natural fear of change, they allow few innovations to take hold in cities. Under the excuse that technology means higher costs, they let excellent projects slip away. Numerous private companies and social organizations develop platforms to help the public sector design policies that actually serve citizens — the people who should be the primary concern of any government or leader.

Governments do, however, embrace technology for one purpose: revenue collection, deploying sophisticated software to extract taxes from citizens. But when it comes to listening to those same citizens, they invest nothing in tools of that nature.

Governments need to reverse the usual flow of information. They are not the ones living through the daily hardships citizens face. Receiving complaints, gathering feedback, and obtaining analytical data on public sentiment are all ways to shift this mindset. The technology already exists; now it depends on the political will of those in power to implement it.
