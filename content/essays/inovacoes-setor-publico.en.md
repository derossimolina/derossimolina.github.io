When we think of entrepreneurship, large corporations or startups with innovative projects usually come to mind. That image stems from the freedom to drive change without the shackles of inertia — a focus on building new products and services to meet society's growing demands.

In the public sector, however, there is little room for innovation, constrained above all by the daily execution of processes often frozen in time. But all is not lost. Public entrepreneurship solutions are emerging in various places, optimizing services, improving how the population is served, and forging new ways to conduct public policy.

In this context, every public servant can become a hub of innovation, contributing to the development of effective public policies through their expertise in their own field. On the other side, companies with modern, technology-driven ideas can optimize services and deliver improvements for the entire population — especially the poorest — by developing alternatives to existing models.

Yet none of these efforts will matter without the direct involvement of the most important figure in municipal government: the mayor. It is through the head of the executive branch that the foundational ideas are built to unite these actors in the search for public policy solutions. Without the political will of the public administrator, whoever they may be, we will remain forever bound to outdated approaches to solving society's real problems.
