Few weeks in Brazil's recent history have been as unsettling as the last. Faced with unilateral sanctions imposed by the United States on our country --- simply because we upheld our own legislation and exercised our judicial self-determination --- we stand at a crossroads: either we reaffirm our sovereignty with dignity, or we bow to the imperial logic that treats us as a protectorate.

This is no exaggeration. Nor is it political reductionism. Some try to disguise the gravity of the moment under the cloak of "ideological polarization," or to attribute it all to a supposed radicalization of society. That is dishonest and dangerous. What is at stake is the continuity of an independent national project or the transformation of Brazil into a satellite subservient to the geopolitical interests of another nation.

The historical parallel that imposes itself, uncomfortable as it may be, recalls the years preceding the Second World War. In the 1930s, a Germany humiliated by the Treaty of Versailles sought to rebuild its wounded pride. The discourse of a "Greater Germany," promising order and prosperity, paved the way for the rise of an obscure politician who, emerging from the beer halls of Munich, transformed the fragile Weimar Republic into a totalitarian regime. The result was the occupation of nations under the justification of "living space" and a supposed civilizing mission. The world paid an enormous price. France, in 1940, fell without resistance, becoming an occupied territory governed by a collaborationist regime: the infamous Vichy government. Meanwhile, the United Kingdom, under the leadership of a reluctant but courageous statesman, rallied around the idea of national sovereignty. Churchill understood that the matter was not merely about preserving borders, but about protecting the identity, culture, and memory of the British people.

Today, although tanks and boots have not crossed our borders, the logic of occupation presents itself in the form of economic blackmail, ideological imposition, and diplomatic manipulation. Brazil is under siege --- not because it threatens anyone, but because it insists on not abandoning its own law. In the 21st century, we are witnessing the resurgence of gunboat diplomacy, now disguised as "civilized" sanctions.

Those who applaud such measures, under the pretext of "defending democracy," resemble those who, in the France of 1940, preferred to collaborate with the occupier rather than fight for national dignity. They are the tropical Vichys. Those who resist, even at the risk of unpopularity and isolation, represent the true patriots. They understand that sovereignty is not negotiable.

The choice, therefore, is clear. Do we want to be like the British of 1939, who faced an aggressive power alone in the name of freedom? Or like the French collaborationists of 1940, who accepted servitude in exchange for an illusory stability?

The answer will define not only our present, but the future of our nation.
