For every topic discussed, there are always multiple sides. A story never unfolds without more than one narrative. As an exercise, consider: what if the Nazis had won the Second World War? This example shows that history is always told from one angle, leaving countless others out, serving the political and ideological interests of certain dominant groups.

To deny that a war of narratives exists is to limit human thought, to fix worldviews in place, to establish a flawed understanding of life in society, and to create mistaken expectations about the future. Each ideology, acting here as a set of ideas, sees the world in its own way. Stories told through Marxist thought see a constant class struggle; stories told through conservative thought see the world with skepticism; stories told through liberal thought see it as an environment controlled by state oppression. Each school of thought perceives the world differently, and at this juncture we need to examine all sides, choose one, defend its merits, critique others, and disagree with still more.

For years, a single school of thought held sway in the country. This Marxist-rooted perspective has its strengths and its weaknesses. However, by restricting access to other lines of thinking, it became the only one available, and its flaws went unexamined. Telling history impartially is therefore essential --- to prevent the creation of a parallel Brazil with restricted access, one that is distorted by the shadows it inhabits.

In this light, we cannot sort ideas into the shallow dichotomy of good and bad. We need to analyze contexts and ideas, their errors and successes --- something that belongs to any democracy. To limit this access, no matter how much we disagree, is to curtail freedom of expression and information.
