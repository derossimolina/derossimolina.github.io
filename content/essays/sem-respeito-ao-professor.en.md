According to its etymology, the word "professor" means "one who professes" --- that is, the person responsible for imparting knowledge to others. To receive the title of professor is to receive recognition and respect for the knowledge one professes.

In Brazil, however, the word carries a different meaning. Each day, fewer professionals dedicate themselves to the art of teaching and transmitting knowledge. The explanations are many, ranging from a lack of physical infrastructure in schools to pitiful salaries. Yet few mention the prevailing pedagogical model.

Based on a supposed struggle between students, cast as the oppressed, and teachers, cast as the oppressors, a premise has spread across Brazil: that teachers do not want their students to grow intellectually or personally. A resounding lie.

The practical result of this fraudulent thinking has been a rise in functional illiteracy, a growing exodus of talented minds to other nations, and a drastic decline in the quality of education. This environment fosters indiscipline among young people, both inside and outside the classroom.

Sadly, thanks to this reigning pedagogical model, teaching in Brazil has been reduced to a mere circus act, where students are a disinterested audience and the teacher a poorly paid jester.

An urgent reform of the prevailing pedagogical orientation is needed --- one that restores the fundamental role of the teacher, making them once again the catalyst of knowledge and steering our country toward a standing common among developed nations. And all of this must be done before we become a nation of fools.
