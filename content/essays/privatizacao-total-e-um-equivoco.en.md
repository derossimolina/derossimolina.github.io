In recent years, with the resurgence of liberal and conservative thought in Brazil, the rallying cry "privatize everything" has become something of a slogan. While the sentiment is understandable — given that the Brazilian state meddles in virtually every aspect of daily life — the expression is something of an exaggeration. There are sectors where privatization would inflict real harm on society.

The economist Milton Friedman, in his book *Capitalism and Freedom*, argues that in certain sectors voluntary exchange is impossible or technical barriers prevent the market from functioning. In those cases, three alternatives exist: private monopoly, public monopoly, or state regulation. It is worth noting that all three are chosen as the lesser evil, not for their virtues.

The worst of the three is state regulation, because it dictates how companies must operate, where they may serve, and what prices they may charge — effectively blocking real competition. This distorts the market: consumers believe competition exists when in fact it does not, producing an oligarchy in all but name.

The lesser evil is private monopoly, where a single player controls the market and can set prices at will. Without rules to limit its reach, such a company can drive up costs across society and undermine freedom of choice, reducing the customer to a mere user with no bargaining power.

At the bottom of the list is public monopoly, where the state provides services to the population by purchasing them on the public's behalf. It remains the least harmful option because, although charges will still apply and customers are treated as users, when prices rise abusively citizens can protest against the government that holds the monopoly.

All that said, there are sectors where voluntary exchange is perfectly feasible. In those cases, privatization is not only necessary but imperative, since it allows society to participate directly in the market, without intermediaries. However, indiscriminate privatization — carried out without weighing the pros and cons in light of the principles of liberty and the circumstances at hand — is the worst mistake a public administrator can make.
