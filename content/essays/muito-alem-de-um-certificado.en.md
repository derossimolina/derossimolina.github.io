Max Gehringer, a celebrated speaker and business consultant, once made a statement back in 2008 that left many university graduates uneasy: technical and vocational programs shorten the distance to landing a job. His reasoning was far from arbitrary — most companies need people with hands-on professional training to perform specific functions, something a typical college graduate often lacks.

The difference lies in how instruction is delivered in vocational programs: they are focused on the market. University education, by contrast, centers on academic learning. As a result, a college graduate may struggle for years before finding employment, often in a field entirely unrelated to the career they originally chose.

On top of that, vocational programs are shorter than a conventional degree. On average they last about a year, while a university degree takes at least five years, if you are lucky. Programs like Information Technology or Business Administration technician courses are sought after by employers because their students acquire knowledge comparable to what a university offers, but with a focus much closer to real-world practice.

Another advantage is cost: while a university degree runs into thousands of reais, a vocational program is far more affordable, making it possible for people with limited financial resources to earn a credential.

"But Professor, a university degree leads to higher earnings," you might say. That is not untrue. But those gains materialize over the long run — ten years or more. A technical professional's career tends to rise more quickly because they already possess the practical tools to work, unlike the college graduate.

If you want to enter the job market armed with practical knowledge, you should seriously consider a vocational program before pursuing a degree. And if you have already completed a degree, a vocational program can add the hands-on expertise to complement the academic foundation you already have.

So what are you waiting for?
