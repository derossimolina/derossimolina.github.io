In the late 1990s, Pearl Jam released a song that spoke directly to the times: "Do the Evolution." The lyrics depict how we have evolved toward cruelty — destroying the environment, stealing in the name of causes, killing in the name of faith.

In other words, the song suggests that humanity cannot be the center of everything; instead, it must share that center with yesterday, today, and tomorrow. It is at least curious that a song can convey something so profound, but its origins lie in Daniel Quinn's philosophical novel *Ishmael*.

We might characterize the pinnacle of human-centered evolution as something close to individualism. Unlike individuality, where each person is responsible for themselves and their actions, individualism pushes others in the opposite direction — refusing accountability for one's own deeds.

Stripped of empathy, the individualist falls back on the primal drive described by the concept of *homo economicus*, directing all actions solely toward personal satisfaction. Guided only by reason, aiming "to get the most with the least effort," the individual stands entirely alone in this endeavor.

Roger Scruton, in his book *How to Be a Conservative*, argues that the posture of the human being as a social creature is to be a participant in community. Those who are not embedded in community will have their way of life overtaken by the individualism of economic man.

Unlike the socialist or internationalist concept, community is not the collective mass guided by state power that we mistakenly call "society." We can only speak of "we" when we take into account traditions, the human bonds of affection and regard, and the organization of people around their foundations.

In this light, progressives forget past traditions, ignore human relationships, and hand down their directives from on high. An amorphous mass of people without direction marches side by side, mimicking the commands of leaders. The result is the rationalization of life — the trivialization of human relationships, the abandonment of traditions and community life, the disrespect for ancestors, the failure to protect the unborn, the disregard for life itself.

While we do need a market economy oriented toward freedom in the dealings among people, we must also be conscious of the limits we need to preserve. Not everything can be commercialized, as libertarians would prefer. Otherwise, we could put a price on faith, on friendships, on love — on every human act.

And so we must place life, not humanity, at the center of evolution — for it is from life that all of us emerged.
