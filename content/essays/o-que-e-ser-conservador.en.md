While working on a graduate paper, I ventured into conservative thought and, to my surprise, found that it bore no resemblance to what I had been taught in school or at university.

We hold a skewed view of what it means to be conservative. We assume it is about turning back the clock, desperately trying to recover a time that will never return. It would be foolish to think that anyone wants to go back to an era before technology fit in the palm of your hand.

What we can properly call conservative thought is the preservation of traditions and their origins. It has nothing in common with the reactionary, who responds to change by sparking counter-revolutions.

A conservative always respects the individuality of each person and does not meddle in it. They do not want the state regulating social life, whether through heavy-handed legislation or through the oppression of minorities.

However, the conservative wants their right to defend the traditional family to be respected, in the same way that a gay person wants their rights respected. They seek equity in relationships, because above all else, we are all human beings.

A conservative does not believe in politicians who style themselves as "saviors of the nation," nor do they harbor authoritarian tendencies. Yet they refuse to accept authoritarianism dressed up as a defense of "morals and ethics."

Conservative thought, however, is broad and vast: it begins in philosophy, moves through sociology, encompasses education, and arrives at politics. A brief account in a few words can only ever be incomplete. What can be said is that a conservative's primary aim is the constant pursuit of Truth.
