Nothing is worse for an economy than conflict: supply chains grind to a halt, inflation surges, and unemployment advances. That is why South America is a region largely free of major wars, with Brazil historically serving as the balancing force. This regional hegemony is not merely a product of Brazil's size, but of a geopolitical vision of Latin American integration built from the 1930s through the Cardoso administration.

Yet the current international landscape recalls the period before the Second World War. The United States has adopted a big stick posture, seeking to reassert its influence by force. Since September, it has intensified its military presence in the Caribbean under the pretext of combating drug trafficking, without agreement from regional leaders. At the same time, Donald Trump has imposed unjustified tariffs on Brazil. These measures, bordering on sanctions, have disrupted trade and fueled inflation on basic goods within the United States itself, forcing the American government to walk back tariffs on coffee and beef.

Contrary to the official narrative, the sanctions against Brazil are tied to maneuvering in Venezuela. The United States is seeking to reclaim its sphere of influence through economic or military force --- an approach that has recently failed in the Middle East. A conflict in Venezuela would destabilize the entire region, unraveling the integration carefully stitched together by Brazilian diplomacy, while triggering waves of refugees and surging military expenditures.

In a time that demands cooperation for innovation and climate solutions, a conflict would bring nothing but setback, misery, and hardship to all South Americans.
