# Who I Am

Hi,

I'm Jerônimo de Rossi Molina — a doctoral researcher in Administration at the University of Caxias do Sul (UCS/Brazil), where I study complex systems applied to money laundering. Before getting here, I followed paths that might seem scattered at first glance: innovation agent, lecturer, public manager, geopolitics researcher. Over time, I realized that the common thread running through all of it was always the same question: **how does order emerge from chaos — and how does chaos organize itself to escape order?**

# How I Think

My intellectual formation does not follow a straight line. It starts from an uncomfortable premise I learned from Carl Schmitt and Chantal Mouffe: **conflict is not a failure of politics — it is its condition of existence**. Public Administration that ignores this tends to build fragile consensuses where it should be building robust institutions.

From that political foundation, the step toward International Relations was natural. Kenneth Waltz and John Mearsheimer show that the international system operates under the same logic: there is no arbiter above states, only rational actors competing for survival and hegemony. Anarchy is not disorder — it is a structure with its own emergent rules.

And that is precisely where John Holland comes in. Complex Adaptive Systems theory reveals that sophisticated collective behaviors — markets, ecosystems, criminal networks — emerge without central planning. When the state retreats, no vacuum appears: alternative systems of order arise, complete with their own hierarchies, norms, and mechanisms of coercion.

It is at this intersection of conflict, system, and emergence that I situate my research.

# What I Research

My dissertation investigates **money laundering schemes as complex adaptive systems**. Criminal organizations that launder assets do not operate with a visible central command. They are distributed networks, where multiple agents make local decisions — and the collective result is a highly effective, resilient system that is difficult to map using conventional investigative and regulatory tools.

To study this, I use **Generative Agent-Based Modeling (GABM)** — a computational approach that simulates how agents with different profiles, incentives, and information interact and produce emergent collective patterns. The goal is not merely to describe how money laundering works, but to **predict behaviors under different regulatory scenarios**, contributing to more effective Anti-Money Laundering (AML) policies.
